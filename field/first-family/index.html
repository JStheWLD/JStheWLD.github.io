The First Family
of American Roots MusicFive generations · one hundred and fifty years

One family that kept walking into the places a sound was about to disappear from, a cattle trail at night, a prison yard, a cotton field, a front porch, and caught the voice before it was gone.

You already know the songs. You never knew the name.

Where does a song come from?

Nobody writes down the first one. It sits in the air of a place, passed hand to hand and mouth to mouth, older than anyone alive to sing it, and it lives in the body wherever the body goes. A song like that can't be owned and it can't be kept, and for most of human history it couldn't be saved either. It could only be caught, by somebody standing there on the day it was still being sung, with a machine that listened.

The Lomaxes made that their family trade.

Generation OneJohn A. Lomax

John Avery Lomax was born in 1867 beside a branch of the old Chisholm Trail, close enough to hear the cowboys singing the cattle quiet in the dark. He carried those songs to the University of Texas in a roll of pencil manuscript tied with cotton string, and a professor looked them over and called them cheap, unworthy of a serious man. So that night, out behind the dormitory, he burned every scrap.

It took him ten years and a spell at Harvard to learn the professor was wrong. When he finally went back out for the songs, he carried an Edison cylinder machine, and this time he wasn't after his own. He was after everybody's. In the summer of 1933 he drove through the gate at Angola and set that machine down in front of a man named Huddie Ledbetter.

Generation TwoAlan Lomax

Alan Lomax was fifteen the first time he went out collecting with his father, and he never really came back in. Where the old man had gathered the songs of one country, the son set out to gather the songs of the whole world. He recorded work gangs at Parchman Farm and fishermen in the Caribbean, shepherds in Italy and miners in Scotland, tens of thousands of performances by people whose names had never been written down anywhere.

He had a word for what he was fighting. He called it the cultural grey-out, the slow flattening of the world's many musics into a few loud ones sold everywhere. Against it he set a principle he named cultural equity, the plain and radical idea that every people's song had a right to survive.

Generation TwoJohn A. Lomax Jr.

The son who drove is easy to overlook. When John Lomax hit bottom in the early thirties, broke and grieving and past sixty, it was his oldest boy, John Junior, who loaded him into a car and pointed it back toward the work. He is the reason there was a comeback to have.

He made his own life in Houston, where in 1951 he helped start a folklore society that was really just a room where people could sing. He managed the bluesman Lightnin' Hopkins. And that plain room, over the years, gave a first stage to Townes Van Zandt, Guy Clark, Lucinda Williams, Nanci Griffith, and a young Steve Earle. A family that recorded the last century of American song also quietly raised some of the people who would write the next one.

Generation TwoBess Lomax Hawes

Bess was the youngest, and she carried the songs into the halls of government. In her twenties she sang in the Almanac Singers alongside Pete Seeger and Woody Guthrie, and in 1949 she co-wrote a little tune about a man trapped forever on the Boston subway. The Kingston Trio turned it into a hit ten years later, and "Charlie on the M.T.A." is still sung by people who have no idea a Lomax wrote it.

Later she ran the folk arts division of the National Endowment for the Arts for fifteen years, and built the program that honors the country's traditional artists while they are still alive to feel it. In 1993 the President hung the National Medal of Arts around her neck, the second the family had earned, her brother Alan having gotten there first.

Generation ThreeJohn Lomax III

The grandson took the family trade to Nashville. John Lomax the Third did for country singers what his grandfather had done for cowboys and his uncle had done for prisoners, catching more than a hundred of them talking for the Country Music Foundation, George Strait and Dwight Yoakam and Lyle Lovett among them, some before the world knew their names.

But he did more than catch them. He managed Townes Van Zandt when almost nobody was listening, got Steve Earle his first record deal, stood backstage at Gram Parsons' last Houston shows with a camera, and brought a young Australian named Kasey Chambers across the ocean to America. In December of 2025, at eighty-one, he became the first Lomax ever to stand on the Grand Ole Opry stage and sing the old songs himself. The line did not stop. A hundred and fifty years in, somebody in the family is still holding the machine.

Act Four · the reckoning

Who gets paid for a voice

In 1959 Alan Lomax recorded a Mississippi prisoner named James Carter leading a work gang through a song called "Po' Lazarus" while they chopped logs. Forty-three years later that recording opened the soundtrack to O Brother, Where Art Thou?, a record that sold eight million copies and won Album of the Year. Lomax's daughter tracked Carter down to a house in Chicago and put a twenty-thousand-dollar check in his hand. He had forgotten he ever sang it.

Not everyone got found. On a David Guetta record with over a billion views, and on a Beyoncé record about American freedom, the voice underneath belongs to a man known to the archive only as a prisoner number. The catch keeps paying out. The question of who it pays has never once been simple.

A song cannot be searched and taken at the gate. It turns out it cannot always be credited at the door either.

Act Four · and now

The immeasurable, at a new hinge

Late in his life, dreaming up a database that could hold every song he had ever gathered, Alan Lomax wrote that it might one day become rich enough, with the help of artificial intelligence, to discover things no person had thought to ask. He wrote that in the early nineteen-nineties, about the machines we are only now learning to live beside.

For a hundred and fifty years the Lomaxes ran toward the vanishing, carrying a machine that could catch a voice before the world it came from was gone. We are standing at another one of those hinges now, a moment when a great deal of what makes us human is being measured, copied, and sold.

The songs are still on the shelf, up there with the dreams and the prayers, the things you can't quite hold and can't quite let go of. Somebody still has to keep them.
